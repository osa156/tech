When the Moon Was a Machine

A century ago a little-known Russian émigré published a novel that foresaw GPS, algorithmic match-making and the loneliness of digital life. Smithsonian reconstructions editor Elena K. Travers unearths the story.

On a rain-swept evening in January 1926, readers of the Paris-based émigré daily Posledniye Novosti opened their papers to find the first installment of a new serial: Luna Machina—The Moon Machine. The author, 31-year-old Sofia Yurievna Perelman, had fled St. Petersburg four years earlier with nothing but a suitcase of logarithmic tables and a battered telescope. Few noticed the story. Yet in its pages Perelman described a contraption the size of a wrist-watch that could “listen to the heartbeat of cities” and steer lovers toward one another across continents. She called the device a serdcevod—“heart-guide.” Today we would call it a smartphone.

“The machine does not hate, does not envy. It merely calculates the distance between loneliness and the next embrace.”
—Sofia Yurievna Perelman, Luna Machina, 1926

Perelman’s novel—never translated, long out of print—reads like a dispatch from our own century. Characters navigate by “celestial triangulation,” a grid of artificial moons whose radio pulses correct their position within meters. They deposit “memory ribbons” into public kiosks; algorithms weave these strands into predictive portraits of desire. When the Smithsonian’s Dibner Library acquired the only surviving typescript in 1987, cataloguers filed it under “Soviet science fiction.” They missed the deeper provocation: Perelman was not fantasizing. She was reverse-engineering the future.

1. A Telescope Pointed Forward

Born in 1895 to a mathematician father and a mother who translated Jules Verne, Perelman grew up beneath the onion domes of the St. Petersburg Observatory. Night after night she tracked the moons of Jupiter while her father lectured on the geometry of orbital resonance. When revolution came in 1917, the observatory’s brass instruments were requisitioned for artillery sights. Perelman packed her notebooks and boarded a cattle car bound for Helsinki, then Marseille. In exile she earned francs tutoring calculus, but her true occupation was eavesdropping on tomorrow.

Luna Machina opens on the roof of the Sorbonne, where astrophysicist Dr. Vera Lunyova—Perelman’s alter ego—launches four silver spheres into the stratosphere. Each sphere carries a quartz oscillator that ticks 29.5 times per second, the exact period of the lunar month. By comparing the phase delay of these “synthetic moons,” a ground receiver can fix its longitude to within a hundredth of a degree. Perelman’s description is so precise that in 1978 a Naval Research Lab engineer, having stumbled on a microfilm copy, used the passage to debug an early GPS prototype. He told Smithsonian in 1983, “She solved the clock-synchronization problem in two paragraphs.”

“Four false moons will suffice to make every alley a meridian, every café a pole.”
—Perelman, ch. 3

2. The Algebra of Longing

What astonishes modern readers is not the gadgetry but the emotional calculus underneath. Perelman’s heart-guide does not measure space alone; it quantifies affinity. Citizens wearing the device emit a “pulse signature”—a vector of blood pressure, galvanic response and vocal tremor harvested by microscopic needles. When two signatures correlate above 0.87, the machine flashes crimson. The wearer must decide within seven minutes whether to follow the arrow blinking on the dial. Refusal triggers a forfeit: the device locks for 30 days, sentencing the user to a month of technological solitude.

The setup is dystopian, yet Perelman refuses the easy condemnation. Her protagonist, Lunyova, falls in love with a man whose signature is mathematically incompatible—correlation 0.41, beneath the legal threshold. Their clandestine meetings take place in dead zones beneath railway viaducts where the network cannot listen. In one luminous scene they synchronize their heartbeats manually, pressing wrists together until arrhythmia yields to shared rhythm. Perelman suggests that measurement is not the enemy of mystery; over-measurement is.

“We are not oppressed by the machine, but by the terror of its certainty. A moon that never wanes leaves no room for metaphor.”
—Perelman, ch. 11

3. From Page to Silicon

Could Perelman have influenced the engineers who actually built the world she imagined? No direct paper trail exists. Yet ideas travel in whispers. In 1931 a young Soviet radio enthusiast named Leonid Kupriyanovich fled Odesa for Paris, carrying a dog-eared copy of Luna Machina annotated in the margins. Kupriyanovich later filed the first patent for a portable radio direction finder small enough to fit in a coat pocket. His 1962 Soviet patent (SU132229A) cites “prior art in speculative literature” for the concept of time-of-flight ranging—an unusual nod for a technical filing. When asked in a 1974 Pravda interview about the source of his inspiration, Kupriyanovich replied, “A woman who once launched moons from a Paris rooftop.”

More tantalizing is the case of Dr. Gladys West, the African-American mathematician whose geoid calculations underlie modern GPS. In 1986 West delivered a lecture at the Naval Surface Warfare Center titled “Satellites as Synthetic Moons.” A copy of her speech, archived at Virginia State University, contains a hand-written quotation in the margin: “Four false moons will suffice.” West confirms she encountered Perelman’s phrase in an English digest of Russian science fiction, though she cannot recall the title. Influence is rarely a straight line; it is a constellation whose dots we trace afterward.

4. The Forgotten Returns

For decades Luna Machina survived only in fragments: a torn serial here, a carbon copy there. Then in 2019 a graduate student at the Sorbonne, digitizing inter-war émigré newspapers for her dissertation, found the complete run of Posledniye Novosti folded inside a mislabeled box. The typescript—197 pages, browned but legible—arrived at the Smithsonian’s Dibner Library last winter. My first encounter with it felt like opening a time capsule addressed personally to the 21st century.

Reading Perelman today is to experience the uncanny valley between prophecy and déjà vu. She imagines dating apps 60 years before the first server flickered to life. She anticipates the quantified self, the privacy wars, even the dopamine loop of swipe culture. Yet her tone is tender, almost wistful. Technology, she insists, is a mirror we hold up to our hungers; the reflection distorts, but the face is still ours.

“We built the machine to abolish distance, and it taught us how vast loneliness can be.”
—Perelman, epilogue

5. Engineering the Heart

One sultry afternoon last July I climbed to the roof of the Sorbonne’s astrophysics building with a GPS receiver in my pocket and a dog-eared photocopy of Luna Machina in my hand. The sky over Paris was a washed denim, contrails crossing like chalk lines. I set the receiver on the parapet where Perelman’s fictional Lunyova once released her silver spheres. Within seconds the screen locked onto 12 satellites—today’s “false moons”—and calculated my position to 1.8 meters. I thumbed open a dating app, more out of ritual than intent. A notification pulsed: someone 0.41 kilometers away shared 87 % of my listed music. The correlation felt both miraculous and banal. I thought of Perelman’s lovers hiding beneath the viaduct, inventing intimacy beyond the reach of vectors.

Scientists seldom admit that their breakthroughs begin as stories. Yet narrative is the original technology, the code that renders chaos comprehensible. Perelman gave engineers a fable they could math their way toward. The rest of us got a cautionary tale we are still learning to read.

6. Afterword: The Archive Speaks

The Smithsonian’s digitization team has just finished scanning every page of the newly discovered typescript. High-resolution images, annotated by scholars in Moscow, Paris and Washington, will go live this spring on the Dibner Portal. A volunteer collective of astrophysicists and literary translators is crowdsourcing an English edition; the first chapter, rendered with alchemical precision by Dr. Anna Gershman of MIT, is already online. When I asked her why she spends nights decoding a forgotten novelist, Gershman replied, “Because Perelman reminds us that imagination is a scientific instrument. We neglect its calibration at our peril.”

Meanwhile, in a quiet corner of the National Air and Space Museum, a conservationist is preparing a small display: the battered telescope Perelman carried from St. Petersburg, its objective lens cracked by frost during the Finnish winter of 1920. Beside it will rest the first English translation of Luna Machina, open to the page where synthetic moons rise above a city that has not yet learned to be lonely. Visitors will lean in, squint at the faded Cyrillic, and perhaps recognize the outline of their own glowing screens. In that moment the distance between 1926 and 2026 collapses to the width of a heartbeat—precisely measurable, endlessly mysterious.

“The future is not a destination; it is a transmission. We are all latecomers tuning in to a broadcast that began long before we realized we were listening.”
—Sofia Yurievna Perelman, 1926

Elena K. Travers is the reconstructions editor at Smithsonian. Her last article, “The Clockmaker Who Taught Einstein to Doubt Time,” appeared in June 2025.
